PizzaBot

September - December 2016

I designed and built the PizzaBot as a member of a group in my Design of Planar Machinery Class, ME 130. Our goal was to automate the repetitive task of slicing a pizza into six even slices. The PizzaBot consists of two primary mechanisms, each powered by DC gear motor. One motor actuates a slider crank mechanism that moves a pizza wheel, supported by a linear slide, across the cutting plate to slice through the whole pizza. After the return cut the pizza wheel moves off the cutting plate for several seconds. During this time, a Geneva mechanism rotates the cutting plate into position for the next cut. After the device has completed all three cuts and is switched off, the entire cutting plate can be removed for easy consumption and cleaning. The system was designed with no need for a microcontroller or other sophisticated electronics.

Slider Crank

The slider crank is powered by a 12V DC gear motor rotating constantly at 6RPM. This speed mimics a reasonable pizza cutting speed and allows an entire pizza to be sliced in 30 seconds.

The two pivot joints are connected with shoulder screws and ball bearings to ensure smooth operation. The pizza wheel is constrained by a linear ball bearing slide to guide its motion and limit additional friction opposing the motor torque.

Geneva Mechanism

The geneva mechanism is powered by the same motor as the slider crank. Using motors with identical speeds ensures that the timing of the two mechanisms remains in sync without a micro controller or more complex feedback system. The pronged geneva wheel is bolted to a Lazy Susan bearing and holds a 3D printed block with pegs with which the cutting plate aligns and sits on.

Analysis

The horizontal force required to cut through a pizza crust was determined experimentally to be approximately 8N. The motor torque specification for the slider crank mechanism was found by performing a Solidworks motion analysis with this force opposing the wheel's motion in both directions. The chosen motors produce up to 4.32 newton-m of torque, giving a safety factor of approximately 1.5 over the peak torque required in the simulation, 2.82 newton-m.

The two links of the slider crank mechanism and the connecting pin were analyzed using Solidworks' FEA. These components were identified because they transmit the largest magnitude torque of all components in the system, and because the links are relatively long and slender. Unsurprisingly, our analysis revealed very little deflection since aluminum and steel are strong materials and the forces in our project are comparatively small.
